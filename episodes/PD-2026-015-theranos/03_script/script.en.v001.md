## ACT I — THE PROMISE — 1:15–3:45
[VO:] It begins in 2003, with a nineteen-year-old who drops out of Stanford to start a company. Her name is Elizabeth Holmes, and her idea is genuinely seductive [CLM-0001].

[VO:] Today, a blood test means a needle, a vial, a lab, a wait. Holmes promised to erase all of that. Her company, Theranos, said its device — called the Edison — could run hundreds of tests from a single, painless drop of blood from your finger, quickly and cheaply, maybe one day in every drugstore and home [CLM-0001]. If it were real, it would change medicine. That "if" is the whole story.

[VO:] And the world wanted it to be real. Money poured in. At its peak, around 2014, Theranos was valued at roughly nine billion dollars, briefly making Holmes a paper billionaire and one of the most celebrated founders in America [CLM-0002]. Its board filled with famous names — former statesmen and military leaders. It struck a deal with the pharmacy chain Walgreens to put its testing in some stores [CLM-0002].

[VO:] And the prestige fed on itself. A board of household names made the company look unimpeachable; a soaring valuation made skeptics look foolish for doubting. Each famous believer became a reason for the next person not to look too closely. That is how a story, more than a product, can carry a company for years [CLM-0002].

[VO:] Notice what made this so powerful. It was not just a product; it was a story — a brilliant young woman, a revolutionary machine, a future where a drop of blood could save your life. Investors, reporters, and partners all wanted to be part of it. And a beautiful enough story can make very smart people stop asking the most basic question: does the thing actually work?

## ACT II — THE CRACKS — 3:45–6:15
[VO:] In 2015, someone finally asked it in print. A Wall Street Journal reporter, John Carreyrou, published an investigation built on internal documents and whistleblowers [CLM-0003].

[VO:] What it reported was devastating. The Edison did not work as advertised. Behind the scenes, the investigation said, Theranos was running many of its blood tests not on its own revolutionary device, but on ordinary commercial machines made by other companies — the very kind of equipment it claimed to have replaced. And the finger-prick results it did produce had serious accuracy problems [CLM-0003].

[VO:] For a tech demo, unreliable results are an embarrassment. For a medical test, they are something else entirely — because real people were making real health decisions based on the numbers [CLM-0003].

[VO:] From there, the fall was fast. Regulators moved in. The Walgreens partnership ended. In 2018, the Securities and Exchange Commission charged Holmes with fraud, and she settled those civil charges without admitting or denying wrongdoing — paying a penalty and accepting a ban from leading a public company [CLM-0004]. That same year, Theranos dissolved. The nine-billion-dollar company was worth nothing.

[VO:] But a collapsed company and a civil settlement are not the same as a crime. The harder question — the criminal one — was still coming. Did Elizabeth Holmes simply fail at something hard while believing in it? Or did she knowingly deceive the people who trusted her? Those are not the same thing, and the difference between them is the difference between a sad story and a prison sentence.

## ACT III — THE TRIAL — 6:15–9:15
[VO:] That question went to a federal court, in the case United States v. Holmes. And to understand the verdict, you have to understand what fraud actually is, legally.

[VO:] Fraud is not failure. It is not even lying in some loose, everyday sense. Criminal fraud requires intent to deceive — proof that a person knowingly made false statements to get something of value from someone who relied on them [CLM-0009]. A founder who genuinely believes in a product that does not pan out has not committed a crime. A founder who knows the truth and hides it to keep the money flowing has. The entire trial was a fight over which of those describes Elizabeth Holmes.

[VO:] At trial, the two sides told that exact split-screen story. Prosecutors argued that Holmes knew about the device's failures and hid them, pointing to internal results and warnings she had received. Her defense argued that she was a true believer who genuinely thought the technology would work, and that being wrong is not the same as lying. The jury's job was to decide, count by count, which story the evidence supported beyond a reasonable doubt [CLM-0009].

[VO:] On January 3rd, 2022, the jury delivered a verdict — and it was not a simple one. It convicted Holmes on four counts of fraud against investors: one count of conspiracy, and three counts of wire fraud, for deceiving specific investors who had put in large sums [CLM-0005]. On those counts, the jury found that she had crossed the line — that she knowingly deceived the people funding the company.

[VO:] But on the counts that accused her of defrauding patients, the jury found her not guilty [CLM-0006]. And on three more investor counts, the jurors could not agree at all, so the judge declared a mistrial on those [CLM-0006]. Convicted on some, acquitted on others, deadlocked on the rest.

[VO:] It is tempting to read the patient acquittals as a verdict that the technology was fine, or that no one was hurt. It is not. An acquittal means only that the prosecution did not prove those particular charges beyond a reasonable doubt — the highest bar in law — not that the underlying conduct was harmless [CLM-0010]. One juror later suggested the patient link was simply harder to pin directly on Holmes. And the contrast makes the point sharply: Theranos's former president, Ramesh Balwani — her business and former romantic partner — was tried separately, and he was convicted on all twelve counts, including the patient counts, and sentenced to nearly thirteen years [CLM-0008] [CLM-0010].

[VO:] As for Holmes: in November 2022, she was sentenced to about eleven years and three months in federal prison [CLM-0007]. The young woman once compared to Steve Jobs was now a convicted fraudster — but, precisely, a fraudster of investors, on four specific counts proven to a jury.

## ACT IV — THE LINE — 9:15–10:45
[VO:] So where, exactly, is the line that Holmes crossed? Because here is the uncomfortable truth: a lot of what Theranos did looks like ordinary startup behavior. Founders pitch a future that does not exist yet. They project confidence they do not fully feel. "Fake it till you make it" is practically a Silicon Valley slogan, and most of the time the law leaves it alone [CLM-0009].

[VO:] The line is intent and reliance. Selling an ambitious vision is legal. Knowingly telling people something false — that a machine works when you know it does not — to get them to hand over money they would not otherwise risk, is fraud [CLM-0009]. The jury concluded that, with investors, Holmes had done exactly that.

[VO:] And there is a reason the stakes felt higher here than in an ordinary bad investment. A failed app costs you money. A blood test that gives the wrong answer can cost you a diagnosis. That is why the patient dimension haunted the whole case, even where the specific counts did not stick to Holmes — and why Balwani's conviction on those counts matters to the full picture [CLM-0010].
