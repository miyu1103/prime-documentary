# EP18 FILM BIBLE — *Flash Crash* (PD-2026-018-flashcrash)
### 「寝室から市場を止めた男」 — a 30-minute feature documentary

> **これは “重厚な設計書（フィルム・バイブル）”。脚本全文（§5）を含む。** PDは事実ドキュメンタリー。**「アカデミー賞作品賞級」とは、捏造で劇的にすることではなく、検証済みの事実の上で、構成・ナレーション・映像言語・音設計・感情の弧を映画の水準まで磨くこと。** 嘘で泣かせない。真実の精度そのものをドラマにする。
> **R3（存命・有罪答弁者 Navinder Singh Sarao）。** 公開前に法務レビュー必須。スプーフィング/wire fraud の**有罪答弁は事実**として提示可。だが**暴落の因果は「寄与/悪化させた/不均衡に大きく関与」と慎重に帰属**し、**「彼が単独で市場を壊した」とは決して言わない**（§10）。本人の肖像・実写・ディープフェイクは不可（象徴のみ）。

## 0. ステートメント — なぜこれが作品賞級になりうるか

偉大な作品は、小さな具体を通して大きな真実を語る。ここには稀有な素材がある——**金に一切興味のない一人の男が、地球上の誰よりも巨額の金を動かし、世界で最も合理的だと思われていた仕組みを、たった36分、幻で揺らした。** そして我々が問うのは犯人捜しではない。**「壊れたのは彼か、それとも、彼が指で触れただけで崩れた“市場”という機械の方か」**。

この作品の品格は3つの規律から生まれる：
1. **抑制**。煽らない。数字と沈黙に語らせる。最も激しい瞬間ほど、声を落とす。
2. **公正**。サラオを悪魔化も英雄化もしない。検察・市場・本人を等距離で。因果は記録どおり「寄与」に留める。この誠実さ自体が緊張を生む。
3. **慈悲**。彼の孤独・自閉・無欲を、見世物でなく人間として描く。観客が最後に抱くのは断罪でなく、畏れと哀しみ。

## 1. ログライン / テーマ / 中心の問い

- **ログライン**：ロンドン郊外の実家の寝室で、世界と関わらず画面だけを見つめていた一人のトレーダーが、自作の“見せ玉”プログラムで巨大市場を釣り上げ、釣り落とし——2010年5月6日、その市場が36分で約1兆ドルを失った日、彼の名はやがて「暴落を引き起こした男」として世界に晒される。だが本当に、彼が壊したのか。
- **テーマ**：①**システムの脆さ**（合理の仮面をかぶった機械の市場は、たった一人の幻に過剰反応した）②**孤独と無欲**（金でなく“ハイスコア”を追った少年のような男）③**因果の謎**（誰のせいか、を断定できないことの怖さ）。
- **中心の問い（全編を貫く一本の線）**：**「一人の人間が、寝室から、市場を止められるのか？ もし止められるなら——止まったのは市場の心臓か、それとも我々の“市場は賢い”という思い込みか？」**

## 2. 事実の土台（検証済み・§11出典）

**暴落そのもの**
- 2010年5月6日 14:32 EDT 開始、約**36分**継続。ダウ平均は約**998.5ポイント（約9%）**急落し、その大半を短時間で回復。一時**約1兆ドル**の時価が消失。

**因果（★最重要の機微＝作品の知的核）**
- 2010年 SEC/CFTC 共同報告：大口の基礎的トレーダー（Waddell & Reed）が **75,000枚の E-mini S&P 先物（約41億ドル）** を、価格や時間を顧みず“出来高の9%”を狙うアルゴリズムで売却したことが起点の一つ。
- 2014年 CFTC 学術報告：高速取引(HFT)は**「暴落の原因ではないが、即時性を奪い合うことで悪化に寄与した」**。
- 後の CFTC：サラオは株式市場に波及した先物の**注文不均衡に「少なくとも significantly responsible（大きく関与）」**し、暴落を**「悪化させた(exacerbated)」**。→ **断定は「寄与/悪化/大きく関与」まで。単独原因とは言わない。**

**サラオ**
- 英ロンドン西部 **ハウンズロー**の実家の寝室で取引。市販ソフトのコードを改変し、**約定させる気のない大量注文を自動で出しては取り消す「スプーフィング」**を、E-mini S&P500先物（CME）で **2009年〜2014年** に反復。
- **自認 ≥$12.8M**（スプーフィングによる不正利得）。CFTCは E-mini取引で**総額>$40M**と主張。
- 2015年 米司法省が訴追（wire fraud / commodities fraud / spoofing）、ロンドンで逮捕、引き渡し争い。
- **2016年11月10日、有罪答弁**（wire fraud 1件・spoofing 1件）。約**$12.9M**の没収に同意。
- 稼ぎの**大半を投資詐欺（ポンジ含む）で喪失**（報道では£40m超を詐取されたとも）。
- **重度のアスペルガー（自閉症スペクトラム）**。専門家の鑑定：「彼が金に何の関心も用途も持たず、コンピュータゲームのハイスコアを更新する子どものように強迫的に取引したことは、彼の自閉と完全に整合する」。
- 当局に**全面協力**。**2020年1月29日**、Virginia Kendall 判事（シカゴ）が**実刑なし＝在宅拘禁(home incarceration)1年**を言い渡す（最大で約8年に直面しうる中での寛刑）。
- 決定版書籍：Liam Vaughan *Flash Crash*（2020）。

## 5. ナレーション脚本（en・v001・present tense・cinematic / 全文）

> 表記：`[VO]`＝ナレーション（読み）。`[ ]`＝映像/音の演出ト書き（読まない）。**R3：本人肖像なし＝象徴。因果は「contributed / exacerbated / significantly responsible」止まり。**

### COLD OPEN — 36 MINUTES  (0:00–2:30)

[Black. A single green cursor blinks. Then numbers — a price ticker — begin to scroll, calm, ordinary.]

[VO] Two thirty-two in the afternoon. May the sixth, twenty-ten. For most of the world, nothing is happening.

[The numbers tick down. Slowly at first. Then faster. A wall of red.]

[VO] And then, in the space of a few minutes, the largest market on earth begins to fall — not drift, not slide — *fall*, as if the floor underneath it has been taken away.

[Archival-style audio collage, no faces: trading-floor voices rising — "what is this—", "is that real?", "somebody's gotta—". A symbolic graph plunges.]

[VO] Nearly a thousand points, gone. Around a trillion dollars in value, gone. Shares in some of the most famous companies in America trade, for a heartbeat, at a single penny.

[Silence. The red bottoms out. Then, impossibly, begins to climb back.]

[VO] And then — almost as fast as it fell — it comes back. The whole thing lasts about thirty-six minutes. By the end of the day, the experts will tell you the market is fine. The machine corrected itself.

[Beat. The cursor blinks alone again.]

[VO] But a question has been let loose that will take years to answer. Because something moved that market. And when they finally go looking for what — they will find a man, in a bedroom, in a suburb of London, who did not even like to leave the house.

[Title card, stark: **FLASH CRASH**. Cut to silence.]

---

### ACT 1 — THE MACHINE  (2:30–6:30)

[VO] To understand what happened in those thirty-six minutes, you have to forget what you think a stock market looks like.

[Symbolic imagery: not a trading floor of shouting people, but a dark ocean of moving light — data.]

[VO] There are no men in jackets waving paper. There hasn't been, not really, for a long time. The modern market is not a place. It is a network of machines, in locked rooms, trading with other machines — buying and selling not in seconds, but in millionths of a second, far faster than any human eye can follow.

[VO] They are called high-frequency traders. Algorithms. And most of the time, they do something genuinely useful: they stand ready to buy when you want to sell, and to sell when you want to buy. They are the water the market floats on. Economists have a word for it — *liquidity*. The promise that there is always someone on the other side of your trade.

[VO] But here is the quiet truth underneath that promise. These machines are not loyal. They feel no duty to be there. They are reading the order book — the running list of who wants to buy and sell, and at what price — and they are reacting to it, instantly, automatically. Feed them a picture of the market, and they will move.

[Beat.]

[VO] Which raises a dangerous question, one almost nobody was asking out loud in twenty-ten. *What if the picture were a lie? What if you could show the machines something that wasn't true — and let them do the rest?*

[The ocean of light ripples, uneasily.]

[VO] In a bedroom in west London, one man was not asking the question. He was already living inside the answer.

---

### ACT 2 — THE BOY IN THE BEDROOM  (6:30–11:30)

[Symbolic: an ordinary suburban house. A small upstairs room. Curtains drawn against the afternoon. We never see his face — only hands, screens, the back of a chair.]

[VO] His name is Navinder Singh Sarao. He grows up in Hounslow, under the flight path of Heathrow, the planes passing overhead every ninety seconds. He is brilliant with numbers and uneasy with people. Years later, doctors will diagnose him with severe autism.

[VO] He learns to trade not in a bank, but on a screen, and he turns out to be extraordinarily good at it — fast, fearless, able to read the flow of the market like a current in a river. He could have had the office, the city, the noise. He doesn't want them. He wants the room.

[VO] So he trades from home. From the bedroom. Often, his mother is downstairs making dinner while, upstairs, her son moves more money in an afternoon than most people will see in a lifetime.

[Beat. This is the emotional center — keep it tender, not gawking.]

[VO] And here is the detail that everyone who meets him will remember. He does not seem to care about the money. He doesn't buy the cars, the house, the life. People who knew him describe a man trading the way a child plays a video game — locked onto the screen, trying, over and over, to beat his own high score. An autism specialist will later put it plainly: his lack of any interest in the money he earned was not a pose. It was who he was.

[VO] But to beat the high score, he wants an edge. And the market, he believes, is rigged — not against him in particular, but by the fastest machines, the ones who always seem to get there first. So he decides to fight the machines on their own ground. He takes a piece of ordinary, commercial trading software, and he begins to rewrite it.

[The screen fills with code. A single function, modified, repeated.]

[VO] What he builds is simple to describe and devastating to watch. A program that can place enormous orders — and then pull them back, automatically, in an instant, before anyone can take them. Orders that were never meant to be real.

[VO] It has a name, this trick. The law will call it *spoofing*.

---

### ACT 3 — THE GHOST ORDER  (11:30–17:00)

[Clean, beautiful explanatory animation. This is the "knowledge-flex" act — the audience must finish it feeling *smart*.]

[VO] Here is how it works. And the genius of it — the cruelty of it — is that, once you see it, you can't unsee it.

[VO] Picture the order book. On one side, everyone who wants to buy. On the other, everyone who wants to sell. The machines are watching this book constantly, and they reason, quite logically: if there is suddenly a huge wall of people wanting to sell, the price is about to drop. Better to sell now, before it does.

[Animation: a modest market. Then a TOWERING stack of sell orders appears on one side — glowing, fake.]

[VO] So Sarao shows them a wall. Using his program, he places sell orders so large they dwarf everything around them — tens of millions of dollars, layered just above the current price. The machines see the wall. They believe it. And they do exactly what their logic demands: they start to sell, to get out ahead of the drop.

[VO] The price begins to fall — pushed, not by real selling, but by the *fear* of selling.

[Beat.]

[VO] And that is the moment. As the price drops toward where he secretly wants to buy, Sarao cancels the wall. In a blink, the orders that frightened everyone simply vanish — because they were never meant to be filled. They were a ghost. And in the space they leave behind, he buys, low, for real.

[The fake wall disappears; a single real green order quietly executes. The price drifts back up.]

[VO] Then he turns the whole thing upside down to sell. Show a wall, move the price, pull the wall, take the profit. Again. And again. And again — for years.

[VO] By his own admission, this earns him at least twelve point eight million dollars. Regulators will say his trading made far more. He is, in effect, lying to the market — not in words, but in orders — and the market, that vast rational machine, believes him every time.

[VO] For most of those years, almost no one notices. A single trader, in a single room, is quietly leaning on the most sophisticated financial system ever built. And the system, it turns out, leans.

[Beat. Turn cold.]

[VO] The question no one has asked yet is what happens if he leans on it on exactly the wrong day.

---

### ACT 4 — THE DAY  (17:00–22:30)

[Return to May 6, 2010. Now we watch it again — but this time we understand. The tension is in comprehension, not noise.]

[VO] May the sixth, twenty-ten, does not begin as a catastrophe. It begins as a nervous day. Greece is sliding toward collapse; there are riots in Athens on the news; the markets are jittery, looking for a reason to be afraid.

[VO] Into that fear, that morning, Navinder Sarao switches on his program. Investigators will later say that on this day he places, and cancels, orders worth billions — a ghost wall, pressing down, for hours.

[VO] And he is not alone. Somewhere else entirely, a large investment firm begins to sell — for ordinary, legitimate reasons — an enormous position: seventy-five thousand futures contracts, more than four billion dollars' worth, fed into the market by an algorithm of its own. An algorithm told to sell fast, without much regard for price.

[VO] Two great weights, then, pressing on the same fragile market at the same moment — one a careless giant, one a deliberate ghost — and all around them, the high-frequency machines, watching the order book, beginning to flinch.

[Build. Let the music and the falling numbers carry it.]

[VO] At two thirty-two in the afternoon, something gives. The machines that promised to always be there — to be the water the market floats on — do the one thing no one planned for. They step back. All at once. To protect themselves, they stop buying.

[VO] And the floor disappears.

[The plunge. Hold on it. Then — restraint — drop to near silence for the worst of it.]

[VO] Prices don't fall; they collapse into the vacuum. With no machines willing to catch them, shares in giant, healthy companies crater toward nothing. For a few seconds, you could have bought a share of a company worth tens of billions for a single penny — not because anything real had changed, but because, for thirty-six minutes, there was simply no one on the other side.

[VO] And then the safeties trip. Exchanges pause. The machines, cautiously, come back. The water returns, and the market floats again, climbing out of the hole almost as violently as it fell in.

[Beat. Quiet.]

[VO] It is over. A trillion dollars has been lost and found inside a single hour. And the most powerful financial system on earth has just learned something it does not want to know about itself: that it can be brought to the edge — not by a war, not by a crash of banks — but by fear, moving at the speed of light, through a market that was never as solid as it looked.

---

### ACT 5 — WHO BROKE IT?  (22:30–26:00)

[The investigation. Cool, forensic, fair. This is the moral spine — handle the attribution with surgical honesty.]

[VO] Afterward comes the autopsy. And this is where the story refuses to be simple — where you have to resist the ending you want.

[VO] The first official report, months later, points at the careless giant: that four-billion-dollar sell program, dumped into a thinning market. It barely mentions a man in London. Later studies look harder at the high-frequency machines — and conclude that they did not *cause* the crash, but deepened it, by demanding to get out first.

[VO] And then, five years on, investigators arrive at the bedroom in Hounslow. They say his ghost wall, that morning, was part of the imbalance — that he was, in their words, significantly responsible for the pressure that helped tip the market over.

[Beat. The crucial distinction — say it cleanly, attribute it precisely.]

[VO] Notice the word. *Contributed.* Part of. Helped to tip. Not: caused. No serious account says that one man, alone, broke the market that day. The honest answer is harder, and more unsettling than a single villain. A careless sell order, a swarm of self-protecting machines, a world already afraid — and yes, a ghost in the order book — all of it, at once, on a market that had quietly become too fast, and too fragile, for anyone fully to see.

[VO] Which leaves the question this whole story has been circling. If a system can be pushed to the brink by a lie told in orders that were never real — then what, exactly, broke that day? The man who told the lie? Or the machine that could not tell the difference between a phantom and the truth?

[VO] The law, in the end, has to choose someone to hold. And it chooses the man.

---

### ACT 6 — THE RECKONING  (26:00–28:30)

[The human fall. Tender, restrained, no gloating.]

[VO] In twenty-fifteen, they come for him. He is arrested in London, and the United States asks for him — the man the headlines now call the Hounslow day-trader who helped trigger a trillion-dollar crash. He fights extradition. He loses.

[VO] And when investigators finally look at what he has left, they find something almost unbearable. The fortune — the tens of millions he won from the machines — is mostly gone. Not spent. *Taken.* The man who could fool the most sophisticated market on earth had himself been an easy mark, handing his money to con men and failed schemes, including an outright fraud. He had beaten the high score, and kept almost none of the points.

[VO] In twenty-sixteen, he pleads guilty — to wire fraud, and to spoofing. He agrees to give back nearly thirteen million dollars. He cooperates completely, helping the authorities understand the very thing he had done.

[VO] At his sentencing, prosecutors do something rare. They ask the judge for mercy. They point to his cooperation. They point to the diagnosis — the severe autism that, an expert testifies, made the money meaningless to him and the game everything. He faced the possibility of years in prison.

[VO] In twenty-twenty, the judge sentences him to no further prison at all — a single year, served at home. In the bedroom, more or less, where it began.

---

### CODA — HIGH SCORE  (28:30–30:00)

[Return to the small room. The screens are dark now. Outside, a plane crosses the sky.]

[VO] The money is gone. The program is gone. The man goes quietly back to an ordinary house under the flight path, the planes still passing overhead every ninety seconds.

[VO] We would like this story to have a villain, because a villain is comforting. A villain means the system was fine, and one bad man broke it, and now the bad man is caught, and we are safe. But that is not quite what happened here.

[VO] What happened is that a lonely man who didn't care about money, playing a game in his bedroom, reached out one finger and touched the great machine of the market — and the machine, for thirty-six minutes, shook. He did not do it alone. But he showed us, more clearly than any report ever could, how little it might take.

[Beat. Final image: the order book, scrolling, calm again. A single ghost order appears — and vanishes.]

[VO] The market is faster now than it was that day. Faster, and more automatic, and more sure of itself than ever. We are told it is rational. We are told it is safe.

[VO] But somewhere in it, still, is the memory of a single afternoon — when the most rational machine ever built looked at a ghost, and believed it.

[Cut to black. Silence. Then a card: the question, not an answer.]

[**ON SCREEN:** *Who do you think broke the market that day?*  — then the channel CTA.]

## 6. 映像言語（モチーフ・象徴体系・ルック・R3）

- **支配モチーフ**：①**“見えない海”＝光の海**（市場＝データ）。流動性＝水。HFTが引くと“水が引く”＝床が消える。②**ゴースト（幻の壁）**＝立ち上がっては消える発光する注文の塔。③**ハイスコア**＝古いゲームの得点表示の質感（彼の動機）。④**90秒ごとの飛行機**＝ハウンズローの時の刻み・反復・孤独。
- **ルック**：抑制された寒色（紺・黒・端末の緑）。山場のみ赤。質感はフィルムグレイン＋端末CRTの微かな走査線。**煽り色（過剰な赤の点滅）を多用しない**＝品格。
- **R3／象徴の徹底**：**サラオ本人の顔・実写・ディープフェイクは出さない**。人物は後ろ姿・手・椅子・部屋の象徴のみ。実在企業ロゴ・実在トレーダーの顔は出さない。市場/取引所は抽象化。AI画像は `symbolic reconstruction` ラベル常時＋AI開示。
- **図解の主役化（§3 山場）**：スプーフィングの仕組みは**手組みアニメ**で“完全に理解できる”レベルに。観客が賢くなった実感＝高評価・拡散の燃料。

## 7. 音設計 / スコア

- **テーマ**：単音のピアノ/シンセが、機械的アルペジオに侵食されていく（人間→機械）。山場は**低域のドローン＋心拍**、最悪の数秒は**ほぼ無音**（恐怖は音量でなく欠落で）。
- **“36分”モチーフ**：時計/メトロノーム/飛行機の通過音を時間の刻みに。
- **4層**：VO最優先＋ダッキング、BGM（章ごと1曲）、SFX（注文の出現/消失＝微かな発光音・吸い込み音）、ambience（端末ノイズ・遠い飛行機）。整音 −14 LUFS / TP ≤ −1。
- **沈黙の設計**：暴落の底・判決・コーダで意図的な無音。**音楽で泣かせず、欠落で考えさせる。**

## 10. R3 / 公正 / 法務ディシプリン（言い回し不変条項）

1. **有罪答弁は事実**：サラオは spoofing と wire fraud に**有罪答弁した**＝断定可。$12.8M自認・$12.9M没収同意も記録。
2. **因果は断定しない（最重要）**：暴落について「**contributed / exacerbated / significantly responsible for the imbalance**」まで。**「彼が（単独で）暴落を引き起こした／市場を壊した」とナレーターが断定しない。** 必ず他要因（Waddell&Reedの大口売り・HFTの引き・ギリシャ危機の不安）と**併置**。
3. **帰属**：因果評価は**報告書/当局/裁判**に帰属させ、チャンネルの断定にしない。
4. **自閉の扱い**：見世物にせず、**専門家鑑定に帰属**して尊厳をもって。
5. **中立**：英雄化も悪魔化もしない。被害（市場の混乱・一般投資家のストップ約定執行等）も矮小化しない。
6. **肖像**：本人・家族・実在トレーダーの顔/実写/ディープフェイク不可。実在企業の名指し非難をしない（Waddell&Reedは「合法な大口売り」と公正に）。
7. **数値**：$1兆・998.5pt・約36分・75,000枚/約41億ドル・$12.8M/$12.9M・>$40M・2009–2014・2016答弁・2020判決＝単位/年/主体を確認し claims に紐付け。
8. **公開前に専用 法務/権利レビュー必須**（R3・最終不可侵ゲート）。
