## HOOK — 36 Minutes — 0:00–2:30
[VO:] Two thirty-two in the afternoon. May the sixth, twenty-ten. For most of the world, nothing is happening. And then, in the space of a few minutes, the largest market on earth begins to fall — not drift, not slide — fall, as if the floor underneath it has been taken away. [CLM-0001]

[VO:] Nearly a thousand points, gone. Around a trillion dollars in value, gone. Shares in some of the most famous companies in America trade, for a heartbeat, at a single penny. And then — almost as fast as it fell — it comes back. The whole thing lasts about thirty-six minutes. By the end of the day, the experts will tell you the market is fine. The machine corrected itself. [CLM-0001]

[VO:] But a question has been let loose that will take years to answer. Because something moved that market. And when they finally go looking for what — they will find a man, in a bedroom, in a suburb of London, who did not even like to leave the house. [CLM-0003]

## OPENING — The Question — 2:30–3:30
[VO:] This is the story of the most mysterious crash in market history — and of the lonely young man whose name would end up attached to it. Over the next half hour: how a market this powerful could be this fragile, what a single trader actually did, and the question the whole case turns on. Did one man break the stock market? Or did he only show us how easily it could break? [CLM-0013]

## BODY

### ACT 1 — The Machine — 3:30–7:00
[VO:] To understand what happened in those thirty-six minutes, you have to forget what you think a stock market looks like. There are no men in jackets waving paper. The modern market is not a place. It is a network of machines, in locked rooms, trading with other machines — buying and selling not in seconds, but in millionths of a second, far faster than any human eye can follow. [CLM-0002]

[VO:] They are called high-frequency traders. Algorithms. And most of the time, they do something genuinely useful: they stand ready to buy when you want to sell, and to sell when you want to buy. They are the water the market floats on. Economists have a word for it — liquidity. The promise that there is always someone on the other side of your trade. [CLM-0002]

[VO:] But here is the quiet truth underneath that promise. These machines are not loyal. They feel no duty to be there. They are reading the order book — the running list of who wants to buy and sell, and at what price — and they are reacting to it, instantly, automatically. Feed them a picture of the market, and they will move. [CLM-0002]

[VO:] Which raises a dangerous question, one almost nobody was asking out loud in twenty-ten. What if the picture were a lie? What if you could show the machines something that wasn't true — and let them do the rest? In a bedroom in west London, one man was not asking the question. He was already living inside the answer. [CLM-0004]

### ACT 2 — The Boy in the Bedroom — 7:00–11:30
[VO:] His name is Navinder Singh Sarao. He grows up in Hounslow, under the flight path of Heathrow, the planes passing overhead every ninety seconds. He is brilliant with numbers and uneasy with people. Years later, doctors will diagnose him with severe autism. [CLM-0003]

[VO:] He learns to trade not in a bank, but on a screen, and he turns out to be extraordinarily good at it — fast, fearless, able to read the flow of the market like a current in a river. He could have had the office, the city, the noise. He doesn't want them. He wants the room. So he trades from home. From the bedroom. Often, his mother is downstairs making dinner while, upstairs, her son moves more money in an afternoon than most people will see in a lifetime. [CLM-0003]

[VO:] And here is the detail that everyone who meets him will remember. He does not seem to care about the money. He doesn't buy the cars, the house, the life. People who knew him describe a man trading the way a child plays a video game — locked onto the screen, trying, over and over, to beat his own high score. An autism specialist will later put it plainly: his lack of any interest in the money he earned was not a pose. It was who he was. [CLM-0011]

[VO:] But to beat the high score, he wants an edge. And the market, he believes, is rigged — not against him in particular, but by the fastest machines, the ones who always seem to get there first. So he decides to fight the machines on their own ground. He takes a piece of ordinary, commercial trading software, and he begins to rewrite it. [CLM-0004]

[VO:] What he builds is simple to describe and devastating to watch. A program that can place enormous orders — and then pull them back, automatically, in an instant, before anyone can take them. Orders that were never meant to be real. It has a name, this trick. The law will call it spoofing. [CLM-0004]

### ACT 3 — The Ghost Order — 11:30–17:00
[VO:] Here is how it works. And the genius of it — the cruelty of it — is that, once you see it, you can't unsee it. Picture the order book. On one side, everyone who wants to buy. On the other, everyone who wants to sell. The machines are watching this book constantly, and they reason, quite logically: if there is suddenly a huge wall of people wanting to sell, the price is about to drop. Better to sell now, before it does. [CLM-0005]

[VO:] So Sarao shows them a wall. Using his program, he places sell orders so large they dwarf everything around them — tens of millions of dollars, layered just above the current price. The machines see the wall. They believe it. And they do exactly what their logic demands: they start to sell, to get out ahead of the drop. The price begins to fall — pushed, not by real selling, but by the fear of selling. [CLM-0005]

[VO:] And that is the moment. As the price drops toward where he secretly wants to buy, Sarao cancels the wall. In a blink, the orders that frightened everyone simply vanish — because they were never meant to be filled. They were a ghost. And in the space they leave behind, he buys, low, for real. [CLM-0005]

[VO:] Then he turns the whole thing upside down to sell. Show a wall, move the price, pull the wall, take the profit. Again. And again. And again — for years. By his own admission, this earns him at least twelve point eight million dollars. Regulators will say his trading made far more. He is, in effect, lying to the market — not in words, but in orders — and the market, that vast rational machine, believes him every time. [CLM-0005]

[VO:] For most of those years, almost no one notices. A single trader, in a single room, is quietly leaning on the most sophisticated financial system ever built. And the system, it turns out, leans. The question no one has asked yet is what happens if he leans on it on exactly the wrong day. [CLM-0004]

### ACT 4 — The Day — 17:00–22:30
[VO:] May the sixth, twenty-ten, does not begin as a catastrophe. It begins as a nervous day. Greece is sliding toward collapse; there are riots in Athens on the news; the markets are jittery, looking for a reason to be afraid. Into that fear, that morning, Navinder Sarao switches on his program. Investigators will later say that on this day he places, and cancels, orders worth billions — a ghost wall, pressing down, for hours. [CLM-0006]

[VO:] And he is not alone. Somewhere else entirely, a large investment firm begins to sell — for ordinary, legitimate reasons — an enormous position: seventy-five thousand futures contracts, more than four billion dollars' worth, fed into the market by an algorithm of its own. An algorithm told to sell fast, without much regard for price. [CLM-0007]

[VO:] Two great weights, then, pressing on the same fragile market at the same moment — one a careless giant, one a deliberate ghost — and all around them, the high-frequency machines, watching the order book, beginning to flinch. [CLM-0007]

[VO:] At two thirty-two in the afternoon, something gives. The machines that promised to always be there — to be the water the market floats on — do the one thing no one planned for. They step back. All at once. To protect themselves, they stop buying. And the floor disappears. [CLM-0007]

[VO:] Prices don't fall; they collapse into the vacuum. With no machines willing to catch them, shares in giant, healthy companies crater toward nothing. For a few seconds, you could have bought a share of a company worth tens of billions for a single penny — not because anything real had changed, but because, for thirty-six minutes, there was simply no one on the other side. [CLM-0001]

[VO:] And then the safeties trip. Exchanges pause. The machines, cautiously, come back. The water returns, and the market floats again, climbing out of the hole almost as violently as it fell in. It is over. A trillion dollars has been lost and found inside a single hour. And the most powerful financial system on earth has just learned something it does not want to know about itself. [CLM-0001]

### ACT 5 — Who Broke It? — 22:30–26:00
[VO:] Afterward comes the autopsy. And this is where the story refuses to be simple — where you have to resist the ending you want. The first official report, months later, points at the careless giant: that four-billion-dollar sell program, dumped into a thinning market. It barely mentions a man in London. Later studies look harder at the high-frequency machines — and conclude that they did not cause the crash, but deepened it, by demanding to get out first. [CLM-0007]

[VO:] And then, five years on, investigators arrive at the bedroom in Hounslow. They say his ghost wall, that morning, was part of the imbalance — that he was, in their words, significantly responsible for the pressure that helped tip the market over. [CLM-0006]

[VO:] Notice the word. Contributed. Part of. Helped to tip. Not: caused. No serious account says that one man, alone, broke the market that day. The honest answer is harder, and more unsettling than a single villain. A careless sell order, a swarm of self-protecting machines, a world already afraid — and yes, a ghost in the order book — all of it, at once, on a market that had quietly become too fast, and too fragile, for anyone fully to see. [CLM-0013]

[VO:] Which leaves the question this whole story has been circling. If a system can be pushed to the brink by a lie told in orders that were never real — then what, exactly, broke that day? The man who told the lie? Or the machine that could not tell the difference between a phantom and the truth? The law, in the end, has to choose someone to hold. And it chooses the man. [CLM-0013]

### ACT 6 — The Reckoning — 26:00–28:30
[VO:] In twenty-fifteen, they come for him. He is arrested in London, and the United States asks for him — the man the headlines now call the Hounslow day-trader who helped trigger a trillion-dollar crash. He fights extradition. He loses. [CLM-0008]

[VO:] And when investigators finally look at what he has left, they find something almost unbearable. The fortune — the tens of millions he won from the machines — is mostly gone. Not spent. Taken. The man who could fool the most sophisticated market on earth had himself been an easy mark, handing his money to con men and failed schemes, including an outright fraud. He had beaten the high score, and kept almost none of the points. [CLM-0010]

[VO:] In twenty-sixteen, he pleads guilty — to wire fraud, and to spoofing. He agrees to give back nearly thirteen million dollars. He cooperates completely, helping the authorities understand the very thing he had done. [CLM-0009]

[VO:] At his sentencing, prosecutors do something rare. They ask the judge for mercy. They point to his cooperation. They point to the diagnosis — the severe autism that, an expert testifies, made the money meaningless to him and the game everything. He faced the possibility of years in prison. In twenty-twenty, the judge sentences him to no further prison at all — a single year, served at home. In the bedroom, more or less, where it began. [CLM-0012]

## ENDING — High Score — 28:30–30:00
[VO:] The money is gone. The program is gone. The man goes quietly back to an ordinary house under the flight path, the planes still passing overhead every ninety seconds. We would like this story to have a villain, because a villain is comforting. A villain means the system was fine, and one bad man broke it, and now the bad man is caught, and we are safe. But that is not quite what happened here. [CLM-0013]

[VO:] What happened is that a lonely man who didn't care about money, playing a game in his bedroom, reached out one finger and touched the great machine of the market — and the machine, for thirty-six minutes, shook. He did not do it alone. But he showed us, more clearly than any report ever could, how little it might take. [CLM-0006]

[VO:] The market is faster now than it was that day. Faster, and more automatic, and more sure of itself than ever. We are told it is rational. We are told it is safe. But somewhere in it, still, is the memory of a single afternoon — when the most rational machine ever built looked at a ghost, and believed it. [CLM-0013]

[VO:] So here is the question we will leave you with. The next time the floor disappears — and it will — who do you think will be holding the ghost? Thank you for watching.
