## HOOK — 36 Minutes — 0:00–2:00
[VO:] Two thirty-two in the afternoon. May the sixth, twenty-ten. For most of the world, nothing is happening. And then, in the space of a few minutes, the largest market on earth begins to fall — not drift, not slide — fall, as if the floor underneath it has been taken away. [CLM-0001]

[VO:] Nearly a thousand points, gone. Around a trillion dollars in value, gone. Shares in some of the most famous companies in America trade, for a heartbeat, at a single penny. And then — almost as fast as it fell — it comes back. The whole thing lasts about thirty-six minutes. By the end of the day, the experts will tell you the market is fine. The machine corrected itself. [CLM-0001]

[VO:] But a question has been let loose that will take years to answer. Because something moved that market. And when they finally go looking for what — they will find a man, in a bedroom, in a suburb of London, who did not even like to leave the house. [CLM-0003]

## OPENING — The Question — 2:00–3:30
[VO:] This is the story of the most mysterious crash in market history — and of the lonely young man whose name would end up attached to it. It is a story about money, but it is not really about greed. It is about a machine so fast and so clever that almost no one fully understood it — and how close that machine came to falling apart, in front of everyone, in the middle of an ordinary afternoon. [CLM-0013]

[VO:] Over the next half hour, we are going to do three things. We are going to show you how a market this powerful could be this fragile. We are going to show you, step by step, exactly what one trader did — a trick with a strange name, that you will understand completely by the end. And then we are going to ask the question the whole case really turns on. Did one man break the stock market? Or did he only show us how easily it could break? [CLM-0013]

## BODY

### ACT 1 — The Machine — 3:30–8:00
[VO:] To understand what happened in those thirty-six minutes, you have to forget what you think a stock market looks like. There are no men in coloured jackets shouting and waving paper. There haven't been, not really, for a long time. The modern market is not a place. It is a network of machines, in locked, climate-controlled rooms, trading with other machines — buying and selling not in seconds, but in millionths of a second, far faster than any human eye could ever follow. [CLM-0002]

[VO:] They are called high-frequency traders. Algorithms. Programs. And in the years before our story, an arms race had broken out among them — not over who was smartest, but over who was fastest. Firms paid fortunes to place their computers physically closer to the exchange, so their orders would arrive a few millionths of a second sooner. In this market, that head start is the whole game. [CLM-0002]

[VO:] And most of the time, these machines do something genuinely useful. They stand ready to buy when you want to sell, and to sell when you want to buy. They are the water the market floats on. Economists have a gentle word for it — liquidity. The quiet promise that there is always someone on the other side of your trade, so that whenever you need to get out, you can. [CLM-0002]

[VO:] But here is the truth underneath that promise, the one almost nobody likes to say out loud. These machines are not loyal. They feel no duty to be there. They are not investors; they do not care what a company is worth. They are reading one thing — the order book, the live, scrolling list of who wants to buy and sell, and at what price — and they are reacting to it, instantly, automatically. Feed them a picture of the market, and they will move on it before a human could even blink. [CLM-0002]

[VO:] And that is the quiet miracle the whole market rests on — and its hidden weakness. The order book is not just data. It is a kind of collective belief, a shared picture of what everyone else is about to do next. The machines trust that picture completely, because trusting it instantly is the only way to stay fast. But a picture can be painted. And a market that runs at the speed of light, on trust in a picture, can be fooled at the speed of light too. [CLM-0002]

[VO:] Which raises a dangerous question, one that, in twenty-ten, almost no one was asking out loud. What if the picture were a lie? What if you could show the machines something that wasn't true — a flood of buyers that didn't exist, a wall of sellers who were never really there — and simply let their own speed and logic do the rest? [CLM-0004]

[VO:] In a small bedroom in west London, one man was not asking the question. He had stopped asking it years before. He was already living, every single day, inside the answer. [CLM-0004]

### ACT 2 — The Boy in the Bedroom — 8:00–13:00
[VO:] His name is Navinder Singh Sarao. He grows up in Hounslow, on the western edge of London, directly under the flight path into Heathrow, where the planes pass overhead every ninety seconds or so — close enough to rattle the windows, regular as a heartbeat. He is brilliant with numbers, and uneasy with people. Years later, doctors will diagnose him with severe autism. [CLM-0003]

[VO:] He does not learn to trade in a bank, or a glass tower in the city. He learns it on a screen, in a back office and then at home, and he turns out to be astonishingly good at it — fast, fearless, able to feel the flow of the market the way a kayaker feels a river, reading the current, the pull, the places where it is about to turn. People who traded near him describe it as a kind of genius. [CLM-0003]

[VO:] And he could have had all of it — the office, the salary, the city, the noise. He does not want them. He wants the room. So he trades from home, from the bedroom in his parents' house, in a tracksuit, the curtains often drawn against the afternoon. And there is something almost unbearable about the picture: downstairs, his mother is making dinner; upstairs, her son is moving more money in a single afternoon than most people will see in a lifetime. [CLM-0003]

[VO:] And here is the detail that everyone who meets him remembers — the detail that makes this a tragedy and not just a crime. He does not seem to care about the money. Not as a pose, not as false modesty. He doesn't buy the cars, or the house, or the life the money could have bought. People who knew him describe a man trading the way a child plays an arcade game — locked onto the screen, hour after hour, trying, over and over, to beat his own high score. An autism specialist will later put it as plainly as it can be put: his lack of any interest in the money he earned was not an act. It was simply who he was. [CLM-0011]

[VO:] But to beat the high score, he wants an edge. And he has become convinced that the market is rigged — not against him personally, but by the fastest machines, the ones that always seem to get there first, the ones that, to him, are skimming a little from everyone, all day long, simply because they are quicker. He doesn't want to join them. He wants to beat them. So he decides to fight the machines on their own ground, with their own weapon: speed. He takes a piece of ordinary, commercial trading software, the kind anyone could buy, and he begins to rewrite it. [CLM-0004]

[VO:] What he builds is simple to describe and devastating to watch. A program that can place enormous orders — and then pull them back, automatically, in an instant, before any human and almost any machine can take them. Orders that look, for a fraction of a second, completely real. Orders that were never, for one moment, meant to be filled. There is a name for this, a name the law will eventually use. They will call it spoofing. [CLM-0004]

### ACT 3 — The Ghost Order — 13:00–18:30
[VO:] Here is how it works. And the genius of it — the cruelty of it — is that once you see it, you cannot unsee it. So watch closely. [CLM-0005]

[VO:] Picture the order book again. On one side, everyone who wants to buy, and the prices they will pay. On the other, everyone who wants to sell, and the prices they will accept. The machines are watching this book constantly, and they reason, quite logically: if a huge new wall of sellers suddenly appears, far bigger than everything around it, then the price is about to drop. The smart move, the safe move, is to sell now — to get out a half-second ahead of everyone else, before the fall. [CLM-0005]

[VO:] So Sarao shows them a wall. Using his program, he places sell orders so large they dwarf everything else in the book — tens of millions of dollars' worth, stacked, layered, just above the current price. The machines see the wall. They believe it, because they have no way not to. And they do exactly what their logic demands. They begin to sell, fast, to get ahead of the drop. And the price begins to fall — pushed down not by anyone actually selling, but by the simple fear that selling is coming. [CLM-0005]

[VO:] And that is the moment. As the price slides toward the level where he secretly wants to buy, Sarao cancels the wall. In the blink of an eye, the orders that frightened the whole market simply vanish — because they were never meant to be filled. They were a ghost. And in the empty space they leave behind, at the low price his own ghost created, he buys. For real. [CLM-0005]

[VO:] Then he turns the entire thing upside down to sell. Show a wall of buyers, lift the price, pull the wall, sell high. Show a wall of sellers, drop the price, pull the wall, buy low. Again. And again. And again — not for a day, but for years. By his own later admission, this earns him at least twelve point eight million dollars. Regulators will say the true figure, across all his trading, was far higher. [CLM-0005]

[VO:] Think about what that means. He is, in effect, lying to the market — not in words, but in orders. And the market — that vast, rational, lightning-fast machine that holds the savings of millions of people — believes him. Every single time. [CLM-0005]

[VO:] And the strangest part is how invisible it all is. There is no broken window, no alarm, no smoke — just numbers, appearing and vanishing faster than thought, on screens in locked rooms. To almost everyone watching the market in those years, nothing looks wrong at all. The wall goes up, the wall comes down, and only the man who built it ever knows it was never really there. [CLM-0004]

[VO:] For most of those years, almost no one even notices. A single man, in a single bedroom, is quietly leaning his whole weight against the most sophisticated financial system ever built. And the system, it turns out, leans. It bends right where he pushes. The only question left — the one nobody has thought to ask — is what happens if, one day, he leans on it at exactly the wrong moment. [CLM-0004]

### ACT 4 — The Day — 18:30–23:30
[VO:] May the sixth, twenty-ten, does not begin as a catastrophe. It begins as a nervous day. On the other side of the world, Greece is sliding toward financial collapse; there are riots in the streets of Athens on the evening news; and the markets everywhere are jittery, on edge, looking for a reason to be afraid. Fear is already in the room. It only needs a push. [CLM-0006]

[VO:] Into that fear, that morning, Navinder Sarao switches on his program. Investigators will later say that on this single day, he places, modifies, and cancels orders worth billions of dollars — a vast ghost wall, leaning on the market, on and off, for hours. [CLM-0006]

[VO:] But he is not alone. And this is the part the simple version of the story always leaves out. Somewhere else entirely, for reasons that are completely ordinary and completely legal, a large investment firm decides to sell — to hedge a position, to protect itself. It is an enormous order: seventy-five thousand futures contracts, more than four billion dollars' worth, and it is fed into the market not by a person, but by an algorithm of its own. An algorithm given one instruction — sell, and sell quickly — without much regard for the price it gets, or the damage it might do on the way down. [CLM-0007]

[VO:] So now there are two great weights pressing on the same fragile market at the same moment. One a careless giant, selling four billion dollars as fast as it can. One a deliberate ghost, leaning and lying. And all around them, the high-frequency machines — the water the market floats on — are watching the order book fill with sell, sell, sell, and they are beginning to flinch. [CLM-0007]

[VO:] At two thirty-two in the afternoon, something gives. The machines that promised to always be there do the one thing no one had planned for, the one thing the whole system quietly depended on them never doing. They step back. All at once. To protect themselves from a falling market, they simply stop buying. And the floor disappears. [CLM-0007]

[VO:] Now prices do not fall. They collapse, into a vacuum. With almost no machines left willing to catch them, shares in giant, healthy, world-famous companies plunge toward nothing. For a few astonishing seconds, you could have bought a share of a company worth tens of billions of dollars for a single penny — not because anything real had changed about that company, not because of any news, but because, for a few minutes, on the whole of the market, there was simply no one on the other side. [CLM-0001]

[VO:] And it is not only numbers on a screen. All over the country, ordinary people have left standing instructions with their brokers — sell, automatically, if my stock ever falls below this price — small, sensible safety nets meant to protect them. In those thirty-six minutes, as prices fall through the floor, those instructions fire, all at once, selling people's savings into the very bottom of the crash, at the worst prices of the day. The panic of the machines becomes, for a few minutes, the real and lasting loss of people who were not even watching. [CLM-0001]

[VO:] And then the safeties trip. Automatic circuit breakers, built for exactly this, begin to fire; exchanges pause; the panic runs out of room. Cautiously, the machines come back. The water returns. And the market floats again — climbing back out of the hole almost as violently as it fell in. It is over. A trillion dollars has been lost, and found, inside a single hour. And the most powerful financial system on earth has just learned something about itself that it would very much prefer not to know. [CLM-0001]

### ACT 5 — Who Broke It? — 23:30–27:00
[VO:] Afterward comes the autopsy. And this is where the story refuses to be simple — where you have to resist, with both hands, the ending you want. Because we want a villain. A villain would be a relief. [CLM-0013]

[VO:] The first official report, months later, points mostly at the careless giant: that four-billion-dollar sell program, dumped by an unthinking algorithm into a market that had grown too thin to absorb it. It barely mentions a trader in London at all. Then other studies look harder at the high-frequency machines, and reach a careful, important conclusion: the machines did not cause the crash — but they deepened it, by all racing for the exit at once, by demanding to get out first. [CLM-0007]

[VO:] And then, almost five years later, investigators trace the ghost back to its source — to the bedroom in Hounslow. They say his wall, that morning, was part of the imbalance that tipped the market over. In their words, he was significantly responsible for the order imbalances that helped trigger the fall. [CLM-0006]

[VO:] How they found him at all is its own quiet story. The ghost had left almost no fingerprints; his orders had simply vanished the instant they did their work. It took years of patient effort — sifting back through the raw data of that single afternoon, through billions of messages — to trace the vanished orders through the noise to one account, and one house, in Hounslow. The most mysterious crash in market history was unraveled, in the end, not by speed, but by someone slow and stubborn enough to keep looking. [CLM-0013]

[VO:] But notice the words they actually use. Part of. Contributed to. Helped to tip. Significantly responsible for the imbalance. Not, anywhere, in any serious account: caused it, alone. Because the honest answer is harder than a single villain, and far more frightening. A careless four-billion-dollar sell order. A swarm of machines all protecting themselves at the same instant. A world already sick with fear over Greece. And yes — a ghost in the order book, pushing at exactly the wrong moment. All of it, together, at once, on a market that had quietly, invisibly, become too fast and too fragile for anyone, anywhere, to fully see. [CLM-0013]

[VO:] Which leaves us with the question this whole story has been circling from the very first second. If a system this powerful can be pushed to the edge of collapse by a lie told in orders that were never even real — then what, exactly, broke that day? The man who told the lie? Or the machine that could not tell the difference between a phantom and the truth? [CLM-0013]

[VO:] The law, in the end, does not get to answer that with a shrug. The law has to choose someone to hold. And of all the forces in that market on that afternoon — the algorithms, the firms, the fear — only one of them was a person, who had made a choice, and broken a rule, on purpose. And so the law chooses the man. [CLM-0006]

### ACT 6 — The Reckoning — 27:00–29:00
[VO:] In twenty-fifteen, five years after the crash, they finally come for him. He is arrested at his parents' house in London — the same house, the same street under the flight path — and the United States asks for him by name: the Hounslow day-trader, the headlines now say, who helped trigger a trillion-dollar crash from his bedroom. He fights the extradition for months. He loses. [CLM-0008]

[VO:] And when investigators finally open up his life and look at what is actually there, they find something almost unbearable. The fortune — the tens of millions he had won from the fastest machines on earth — is mostly gone. Not spent. Taken. The man who could fool the most sophisticated market ever built had himself turned out to be an easy mark: he had handed his money, almost all of it, to con men, to bogus investments, to an outright fraud built to part trusting people from their savings. He had beaten the high score, over and over, for years — and kept almost none of the points. [CLM-0010]

[VO:] In twenty-sixteen, he pleads guilty. Not to causing the crash — to two specific crimes: one count of wire fraud, and one count of spoofing. He agrees to hand back nearly thirteen million dollars. And then he does something the authorities did not have to ask twice for. He cooperates, completely — sitting with them, explaining his own methods, helping the people who caught him understand the very thing he had spent years perfecting. [CLM-0009]

[VO:] There is a temptation, telling this, to turn him into a folk hero — the lone genius who beat the machines at their own game. Resist that. What he did broke the law, and he admitted it; real money moved, and real people, somewhere, were on the other side of it. But there is no honesty in making him a monster either. The truth is smaller, and sadder: a brilliant, lonely man, chasing a number on a screen, who understood the rule he broke better than almost anyone — and never seemed to grasp, or to care, what the number was actually worth. [CLM-0011]

[VO:] And at his sentencing, the prosecutors do something genuinely rare. They ask the judge for mercy. They point to his cooperation. And they point to the diagnosis — the severe autism that, an expert testifies, was woven through the whole story: that made the money meaningless to him, and the game everything; that helps explain a man who could read a global market like a book, and yet could be robbed blind by the oldest tricks in the world. He had faced the real possibility of years, even a decade, in an American prison. [CLM-0011]

[VO:] In twenty-twenty, the judge sentences him to no further prison at all. A single year — served at home. In the bedroom, more or less, where the whole thing began. [CLM-0012]

## ENDING — High Score — 29:00–30:00
[VO:] The money is gone. The program is gone. And the man goes quietly back to an ordinary house under the flight path, the planes still passing overhead, every ninety seconds, regular as a heartbeat. [CLM-0013]

[VO:] We wanted this story to have a villain, because a villain is comforting. A villain means the system was sound, and one bad man broke it, and now the bad man has been caught, and so we are safe. But that is not quite what happened here. What happened is that a lonely man who did not care about money, playing a game in his bedroom, reached out one finger and touched the great machine of the market — and the machine, for thirty-six minutes, shook. He did not do it alone. But he showed us, more clearly than any report ever could, how little it might take. [CLM-0006]

[VO:] And the market is faster now than it was that day. Faster, and more automatic, and more sure of itself than ever — more machines, more speed, more weight resting on the quiet promise that the water will always be there. We are told it is rational. We are told it is safe. But somewhere inside it, still, is the memory of a single afternoon, when the most rational machine ever built looked at a ghost — and believed it. [CLM-0013]

[VO:] So here is the question we will leave you with. The next time the floor disappears — and one day, it will — who do you think will be holding the ghost? Thank you for watching. If you want more stories about the hidden machinery that quietly runs our money, and our lives, this is what this channel does. Subscribe, and we will see you in the next one. [CLM-0013]
