## HOOK — 0:00–0:30
[VO:] A police officer tries to pull you over for something small. You are almost home, so you keep going the last few seconds, pull into your own garage, and reach for the button to close the door. The officer walks up and sticks his foot under the descending door, stopping it, and steps inside — no warrant, no permission [CLM-0005]. Is that allowed? In 2021, the Supreme Court took up exactly this question, and the answer is one of the clearest statements in years about the one place the law still treats as a fortress: your home [CLM-0001].

## OPENING — 0:30–1:15
[VO:] The Fourth Amendment protects a lot of places, but it protects your home most of all. As a rule, the police cannot enter your home without a warrant — that is the baseline, and the exceptions to it are narrow [CLM-0003]. One of those exceptions is an emergency the law calls "exigent circumstances," and a classic example is hot pursuit: an officer chasing a fleeing suspect. The question in this case was sharp and practical. If the police are chasing you over something minor — a misdemeanor — and you slip inside your home, does that chase automatically let them come in after you? Over the next twelve minutes: a garage door, a foot, and the line around your front door.

## ACT I — THE GARAGE — 1:15–3:45
[VO:] The driver is a man named Arthur Lange, in Sonoma County, California. He is doing nothing dramatic — playing his music loudly and honking his horn a few times. A California Highway Patrol officer notices, starts following, and eventually flips on his overhead lights to pull Lange over [CLM-0004].

[VO:] By now, Lange is just about home — roughly a hundred feet away. Instead of stopping in the street, he keeps going the short distance to his house and pulls into his attached garage [CLM-0005].

[VO:] What the officer does next is the whole case. As the garage door comes down, he steps forward and puts his foot underneath it, so it cannot close. The door goes back up. He walks into the garage, starts questioning Lange, and sees signs that Lange has been drinking. Lange is charged with driving under the influence — a misdemeanor [CLM-0005].

[VO:] Now, one thing has to be clear, because it is easy to get backwards. The legal fight that follows is not really about the DUI. The DUI is just what the officer found once he was inside. The question the courts had to answer is the one step before that: was the officer allowed to enter the garage at all — to follow a man into his home over a minor offense, without a warrant — just because that man had not pulled over? [CLM-0006]

[VO:] California's answer was yes, automatically. Under the rule the state defended, whenever a suspect flees — even from a misdemeanor — that flight by itself creates the kind of emergency that lets police enter a home in pursuit. No case-by-case judgment required. Flight in, door open [CLM-0001].

## ACT II — THE FORTRESS — 3:45–6:15
[VO:] To see why that rule troubled the Court, you have to appreciate how strongly the law guards the home.

[VO:] For centuries, the home has been the high ground of privacy law — the place where the demand for a warrant is at its strongest. The idea is old and blunt: the government should not be able to cross your threshold uninvited unless it has either a warrant or a genuine emergency [CLM-0003]. Exigent circumstances are that emergency category. They cover things like someone about to destroy evidence, a person in danger, or a suspect actively escaping. In those situations, waiting for a warrant could mean losing the chance to act, so the law lets officers move first [CLM-0003].

[VO:] Hot pursuit fits that logic when the stakes are high. If a dangerous suspect runs into a house, no one thinks the police must stop politely at the door and go find a judge [CLM-0008]. The hard question is what happens at the other end of the scale — when the thing being pursued is small.

[VO:] And the range of "misdemeanor" really is enormous. It can mean a violent assault — or it can mean jaywalking, an open container, a noise complaint, playing your music too loud. Treating all of those the same, the Court worried, would mean police could enter a home over conduct that is barely criminal at all, simply because the person did not stop in the street [CLM-0007].

[VO:] And here the two sides pull in opposite directions, and both have a real point. On one side: a suspect should not be able to defeat a lawful stop simply by stepping across his own threshold. If running home always worked, every minor offender would just sprint for the door [CLM-0008]. On the other side: the home cannot lose its constitutional protection over a honking horn. If any flight, from any offense, automatically opens your door to the police, then the strongest protection in the Fourth Amendment can be switched off by the most trivial chase [CLM-0007].

[VO:] So the Court had to decide whether "the suspect fled" is, by itself, enough — a flat, automatic rule — or whether it is just one fact among many that has to be weighed each time.

## ACT III — THE RULING — 6:15–9:00
[VO:] In 2021, the Supreme Court rejected the automatic rule. It held that the pursuit of a fleeing misdemeanor suspect does not categorically justify a warrantless entry into a home. Whether the officer may enter has to be judged case-by-case, on the totality of the circumstances [CLM-0001].

[VO:] The principal opinion was written by Justice Elena Kagan. Flight, she explained, is a real and relevant fact — an officer can absolutely consider it. But misdemeanors cover an enormous range, from the dangerous to the utterly trivial, and the emergencies that justify barging into a home simply are not present every time someone fails to pull over. Sometimes there will be a true exigency, and entry will be lawful. Sometimes there will not. The point is that you have to actually look [CLM-0007].

[VO:] Kagan also leaned on history. At common law — the centuries of tradition the Fourth Amendment was built on — there was no automatic rule that any fleeing suspect could be chased into a home. Officers were generally expected to have a real, pressing reason before crossing that threshold. The Court, in other words, was not inventing a new limit on the police; it was refusing to erase an old one [CLM-0007].

[VO:] It is worth being precise about the vote, because it is often described loosely. The result was unanimous — all nine justices agreed to send the case back. But they did not all agree on the reasoning [CLM-0002]. Chief Justice John Roberts, joined by Justice Samuel Alito, wrote separately. They agreed with the outcome here, but they argued for a broader rule: that hot pursuit of a fleeing suspect should generally justify entry, because an officer in a chase cannot safely pause to weigh the seriousness of the offense, and because letting suspects escape into their homes invites exactly that [CLM-0008]. That disagreement is the heart of the case: not whether the home is protected, but how much room an officer gets in the heat of a chase.

[VO:] The Court did not decide whether the entry into Arthur Lange's garage was ultimately lawful. It vacated the decision below and sent the case back, so the lower courts could apply the case-by-case standard to what actually happened that night [CLM-0009].

## ACT IV — WHAT IT MEANS — 9:00–10:45
[VO:] So here is the practical takeaway. If an officer is chasing you over something minor and you make it inside your home, the police do not get an automatic right to follow you in [CLM-0001]. They need either a warrant or a genuine emergency that fits the moment — a real risk that you will hurt someone, destroy evidence, or get away in a way that matters. The seriousness of what you are accused of, and the danger of the situation, are part of the calculation [CLM-0007].

[VO:] That is not a clean, bright line, and the Court knew it. A case-by-case rule means more judgment calls, more disputes, and more situations where reasonable people will argue about whether the emergency was real [CLM-0008]. But the alternative — an automatic rule that any flight opens your door — would have handed away too much of the one protection the Fourth Amendment guards most fiercely. Faced with that choice, the Court protected the threshold.
