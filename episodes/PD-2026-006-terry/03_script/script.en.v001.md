## HOOK — 0:00–0:30
[VO:] You are walking down a sidewalk when a police officer stops you, turns you around, and runs his hands along the outside of your clothes. He has no warrant. He has not seen you commit a crime [CLM-0001]. Most people assume that is illegal. It is not. The rule that allows it was written in 1968, and it began with one detective watching two men pace in front of a store window [CLM-0005] [CLM-0006]. This is the case that drew the line between a hunch and proof.

## OPENING — 0:30–1:15
[VO:] We think of the Fourth Amendment as a simple wall: the police need a warrant, or at least solid evidence, before they can search you. For most of American history, that was close to true. But there is a gap in that wall, and almost every street stop in the country now passes through it [CLM-0001]. Over the next twelve minutes: the ordinary afternoon that created the gap, the standard the Supreme Court invented to fill it, and the quiet cost of letting suspicion — not proof — put a hand on your shoulder.

## ACT I — THE STREET — 1:15–3:30
[VO:] It is October 1963, in downtown Cleveland, Ohio. A plainclothes detective named Martin McFadden, thirty-nine years on the force, is watching the street the way he has watched it for decades [CLM-0005].

[VO:] He notices two men. One walks down the block, pauses at a particular store window, looks inside, and walks on. A short distance away he turns, comes back, and looks into the same window again. Then the second man does exactly the same thing. Back and forth, the two of them, about a dozen trips in all — five or six times apiece [CLM-0006].

[VO:] To most people on the sidewalk, this is nothing. To McFadden, it is a pattern he recognizes. Men do not usually study a single store window a dozen times in the middle of the afternoon. He believes he is watching two men case a robbery — checking the layout, the register, the way out [CLM-0006].

[VO:] He has not seen a crime. He cannot point to stolen goods or a weapon in plain view. What he has is experience and a strong suspicion. Under the ordinary rule, that is not enough to search anyone.

[VO:] McFadden approaches the men, identifies himself, and asks for their names. They mumble a reply. And now he has a decision to make. If these men are about to rob a store, at least one of them may be carrying a gun. If he is wrong, he is about to put his hands on innocent people for no legal reason.

[VO:] He spins one of the men, John Terry, around and pats the outside of his coat. He feels the shape of a pistol. He reaches in and takes it out. A second pistol comes off the other man [CLM-0006].

## ACT II — THE PROBLEM — 3:30–6:15
[VO:] Terry is charged with carrying a concealed weapon [CLM-0007]. And his case exposes a problem the law had been avoiding for a long time.

[VO:] The Fourth Amendment protects people against "unreasonable searches and seizures." By 1963, the usual test for a search was demanding: an officer needed probable cause — a fair, fact-based belief that a specific crime had been committed — and often a warrant signed by a judge.

[VO:] Detective McFadden had none of that. He had not watched a robbery happen. He had watched two men look in a window. If the court measured his pat-down against the full probable-cause standard, the gun was an illegal search, and under the rule from our last episodes, the evidence would be thrown out.

[VO:] But push the other way, and a different danger appears. Police officers approach suspicious people every day, long before a crime is complete. If an officer can never check a person for a weapon until he already has proof of a crime, then the most dangerous moment of the job — the unknown person, the unseen gun — is also the moment the law leaves him most exposed.

[VO:] So the question that reached the Supreme Court was narrow and uncomfortable. Is there a kind of police contact that sits below a full arrest — a brief stop, a quick check for weapons — that the Constitution should allow on less than probable cause? Or does opening that door hand the police a tool they will use on anyone, for almost any reason?

[VO:] The Court understood both sides of that question. It knew the streets were real, and so was the temptation to abuse a softer rule. The decision it wrote in 1968 tried to hold both truths at once, and that is exactly why it has been argued about ever since.

## ACT III — THE RULING — 6:15–9:00
[VO:] In 1968, by a vote of eight to one, the Supreme Court upheld the search [CLM-0002]. The opinion was written by Chief Justice Earl Warren — the same Court that, in these same years, was expanding the rights of suspects across the country [CLM-0002]. That detail matters. This was not a Court looking for ways to help the police. It was a Court that decided the street was a genuine exception.

[VO:] The Court built its answer in two parts. First, the stop. An officer may briefly stop a person when he can point to specific, describable facts that make him reasonably suspect that crime is afoot [CLM-0004]. Not a hunch he cannot explain — facts he can lay out later, the way McFadden could describe the pacing and the window.

[VO:] Second, the frisk. If, during that stop, the officer reasonably believes the person may be armed and dangerous, he may run his hands over the outer clothing — and only the outer clothing — to check for weapons [CLM-0001]. The purpose is narrow: not to find evidence, but to make sure no one gets shot during the encounter.

[VO:] The Court was honest about what this was. A stop is a seizure. A frisk is a search [CLM-0003]. It did not pretend otherwise. It simply held that this limited kind of search could be reasonable on a lower standard than probable cause — a new standard the law now calls reasonable suspicion [CLM-0004]. With that, the gun found on Terry was allowed to stand [CLM-0007].

[VO:] One justice refused to go along. William O. Douglas warned that the Court was giving an officer on the street a power that even a judge did not have — the power to seize and search a person without probable cause. To loosen the rule below probable cause, he wrote, was a long step down a dangerous road [CLM-0008]. His was the single dissenting voice. But it would echo for the next half-century.

## ACT IV — THE COST — 9:00–10:45
[VO:] The "Terry stop" became one of the most common encounters between police and the public in America [CLM-0009]. Every day, officers stop people on a standard that is real but blurry: facts that add up to reasonable suspicion.

[VO:] The strength of the rule is also its weakness. "Reasonable suspicion" depends on judgment, and judgment can be sharpened by training — or bent by bias. In the decades after Terry, some cities used the stop-and-frisk power so heavily that it became the center of a national argument about who gets stopped, and how often, and why [CLM-0009].

[VO:] That argument is not really a disagreement about the 1968 ruling. The Court was careful. It demanded specific facts, not vague feelings, and it limited the frisk to a search for weapons. The problem is what happens when a careful rule meets the pressure of a quota, a bad assumption, or a long day on a hard street.

[VO:] And so Terry leaves us with a permanent tension. We want officers to be able to protect themselves in the seconds before they know who they are dealing with. We also do not want suspicion alone to be enough to stop and handle anyone, anywhere. The line between those two things is thin, and it is redrawn on sidewalks every single day.

## ENDING — 10:45–12:00
[VO:] Terry v. Ohio answered a question about the street: how much can the police do to your body, in public, on less than proof. The detective watched two men, named a real suspicion, and the Court let the gun stand [CLM-0001] [CLM-0007].

[VO:] But almost everything in that case depended on being out in the open — on a man the officer could see, on a sidewalk anyone could walk down. The Fourth Amendment grew up protecting physical things: your house, your pockets, the ground you stand on.

[VO:] So what happens when the police do not want what is in your pockets, but what is on the device inside them? When the thing they reach for is the phone that holds your messages, your photos, your whole life? The next time the Court faced that question, it had to decide whether the rules built for pockets and coats could possibly fit a computer that fits in your hand.

[VO:] That is next time.
